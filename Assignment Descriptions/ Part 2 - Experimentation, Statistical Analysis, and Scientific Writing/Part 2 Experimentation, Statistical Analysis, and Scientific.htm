        Part 2: Experimentation, Statistical Analysis, and Scientific Writing

        Content

        • Part 2 overview

          Learning goals for Part 2
          1. Designing an experiment with human participants as a way to test a hypothesis that follows from a research question.
          2. Conducting an experiment with human participants as a way to test a hypothesis (and experiencing the difficulty of collecting good data, and why you need to think about this).
          3. Analyzing empirical data using statistical techniques.
          4. Writing a scientific report about your system's empirical evaluation and its place in AI.
          The level that we aim to achieve is that you get a first impression of all these facets, that allows you to better understand the most important considerations for all these steps, and where to find further information on these topics. In addition, the project should improve your ability to express your thoughts in writing, and to be able to better interpret other published scientific experimental work. Even further skills can be obtained in other courses (e.g., Experimentation in Psychology, Linguistics, and AI) and in your final thesis project.
          Motivation for the assignment
          In the development of software or AI technology, you typically want to test the performance of the technology that you developed. Such tests can both entail (1) software tests, and (2) human experience tests. Typically, AI products need to score well on both fronts.
          For a software/technological test, you want to assess whether all the system functionality works in the way you designed it. For example:
          • Does the system result in system crashes? This is a count or binary (yes/no) question.
          • Does a search system return its search results faster after some optimization method for searching has been made? Here you could use a statistical test.
          • Do all the features of the system work as intended? For example, in case the software has a graphical interface, does every button press result in the right action? Here you could use a qualitative or quantitative analysis.
          For a human experience test you typically want to test whether the human (end) user of the technology (or other stakeholders such as management or clients) receives benefits of a system change. For example, compared to an old system, does the new system result in a statistically significant difference in:
          • Finding/doing something faster? For example, finding a suitable film to watch on Netflix faster? (output metric: time interval between start of program and start of film)
          • Making fewer errors? For example, do users click on the right icon on a banner? (output metric: frequency of errors)
          • Achieving more of a specific company target objective? For example, are there more product sales, or do people click more frequently on some advertisement? (output metric: number of sales, number of clicks)
          • Having a better affective state? For example, does a human decision support system reduce stress in humans (output metrics: some physiological measure such as skin conductance, or perhaps a subjective measure of mental workload such as NASA-TLX)
          • Being more satisfied with the project? For example, when booking a hotel through a website, do they rate the service of the website higher? (output metric: score on a survey)
          In part 1 of the assignment, you have designed a system, and therefore also focused on the software/technical analysis of it (does it have all required functionality and not break?). You will now test whether your system also significantly improves some aspect of human/user experience in different operating conditions.

          The assignment consists of four parts:
          1. Design: What can be tested about your system within the constraints set by this course? Why is this an appropriate test and what is your hypothesis?
          2. Method: How can you test this hypothesis in an experiment? What are the necessary characteristics of participants, materials, design, procedure, and output metrics?
          3. Statistical analysis: With what test do you test whether there is indeed the hypothesized difference?
          4. Write-up: Write-up your text in various sections.

          Each lab session will place relatively more emphasis on a specific theme. All students are encouraged to write throughout the lab series.
          Planning and feedback
          Although each week one specific theme will be central, it is important to read ahead what is expected of you for all parts. Occasionally, you will be asked to hand-in a short write-up of your progress. These write-ups are mostly meant for documenting your activities and are occasionally used for peer-feedback and/or feedback by the lecturer. Feedback by peers and lecturers on in-between write-ups will be formative. This means that no grade will be given, and that the feedback is intended to help you create a better final product. When asked to give feedback on others, please be critical and constructive. Do not just say that "everything is OK", but instead think of how you can perhaps help them along.

        • Part 2a: Experiment Design

          Research question
          The first step is to come up with a research question that you can test in an experiment. In part 1 you have designed a restaurant recommendation system. In the design you were forced to incorporate specific components, but you’ve also thought about components that you could turn on or off in the system (see assignment 1C). In your final report, you want to reflect on the choices you made for that system and test the impact that turning a component on or off has on human behavior and/or experience, as assessed in an experiment.

          With your group, you should discuss the following: What was your goal for the components that can be turned on or off? Why did you choose that/those component(s)? And how can you test their effect on human behavior and/or experience?
          Some motivation on why you are doing what you are doing typically goes into the introduction of a scientific manuscript.
          Design
          More specifically, you can consider the following aspects in “designing” your experiment, which you can also use in your writing (side-note: look at other papers and in the APA manual regarding what conventions are for reporting, see Appendix A and Appendix C):
          • What was your research question? Can this question be translated into a concrete hypothesis and can that be measured in human performance? For example, in an improvement in response time, accuracy, subjective rating, reduction of errors? The research question will be discussed in the introduction of your report.
          • Who are your participants? How many do you need? Formally you can test this using a so-called power test, or it can be guided by experience and previous literature. For pragmatic reasons, you might want to keep this limited to about 15 to 30 for this assignment (depending on how complicated your design is and how quickly you can collect data). Note that fewer participants might mean that it is harder to detect differences with your statistical test (a “type II error”). Think also about how you will recruit your participants. Can these be your fellow classmates? Can it be friends? Make sure that participants know what it is expected of them before they do the task (i.e., how you instruct them), but also ensure that they don’t know what the exact test is, so they are not biased to give specific (desirable) answers. The information on participants is discussed in the participants subsection of your methods section.
          • N.B.: For a “real” experiment you also have to receive approval by an ethics board and extensive consent procedures. These are very important for research, but a full ethical approval process will not be used during this course due to time constraints. Please be considerate and respectful of your participants and do not place them in any situation that can be or lead to (future or present) discomfort. Please also treat their data confidentially and in an anonymized way.
          • What is the task that participants do? You’ve thought about this for part 1 of the assignment, but you describe the task and materials again briefly in the materials subsection of your methods section.
          • A typical experiment has some form of an intervention or manipulation. The intervention will be discussed in the design subsection of the methods section of your report.
            • For statistical reasons it is often useful to just have 1 intervention or change with two options that only differ on one aspect. In that way you can compare two groups, and if you find a difference you know which groups were different (you do not always know this with 3 or more groups). So, what could be your intervention? Which features can you turn on or off, and what is the most crucial one that you want to test? Are you sure that this change only affects 1 aspect of the software and not multiple (if it impacts multiple you know that when you find a difference that this difference exists, but you can not explain WHAT caused this difference)? For example, an intervention could be that one group drinks coffee, and another group drinks tea, to test whether drinking coffee improves performance.
            • Often in experiments, one of the two conditions is the "baseline" or "control" condition, and the other is the actual intervention (or: treatment) condition. What do you use?
            • Statistically, it is often beneficial to run a within-subjects experiment in which participants experience all conditions, and between participants you control the order of experience (counterbalancing). You do need to be careful that a previous experience does not spill over to later conditions (“strategy transfer”) and thereby confounds your measurements.
          • Typically experimental measurements are "noisy" due to various factors such as variation between participants (we all differ in our preferences, in our alertness, in our moods, etc) and within the participants (they might get tired over time, or occasionally not pay attention) and inaccuracies in the measurement (not everything might be measured exactly when you thought it would). Therefore, many experiments measure human performance over multiple trials or time-points, and later analyze average human performance. Can you conduct multiple trials without biasing the response of the user?  How many trials do you need to compensate for the noise in your measurement? This will be reported in the procedure section of the methods section of your report.
          • What measures do you take exactly? How do you log this? Is any preprocessing of the data needed? How do you average it and how do you conduct statistics on it? Can you do a simple statistical test (such as a t-test) or is more needed? For the context of this course, you should favor simple tests on data that requires little pre-processing. For a within-subjects design with just 2 conditions, a typical test might be a two-sided paired t-test (paired here meaning that for each participant you have a “pair” of data points from each condition). This will be reported in the measurements section of the methods section of your report.
          • How would you go through the empirical cycle? (that is: how does the real world and literature influence your hypothesis, how does this impact your method, your test, and the impact on your hypothesis and the real world?). This will affect multiple sections of your report.

        • Part 2b: Method implementation and data collection

          Based on your design and write-up of the design, you should now be able to implement the experiment such that you can collect data quickly and carefully. Recall that the purpose of this assignment is for you to gain experience with running an experiment. So, not everything will be perfect, and the lecturers know this when they assess your assignment. However, try to run your experiment as good and professional as possible.

          Make sure that your experiment logs the data that you need and that you store it in a way that you can process and analyze it. Consistent with the perspective that was given in the class on experimentation, and the paper by Cairns (2016), you already know that you should think about how you will analyze your data (step 2C) before you collect the data.

          Note on safety and hygiene (esp. in context of COVID-19) and data collection
          Please be cautious for safety and hygiene when collecting data, especially in the context of COVID-19. Follow the guidelines of the RIVM (Dutch National Institute for Public Health and the Environment, https://www.rivm.nl/en) and any further national, local, or university regulations. Keep your health and that of your participants in mind. Please also think more generally about good hygiene practice.
          Make sure to follow general guidelines and encourage them to not come to the experiment location if they (or someone close to them, such as housemates) show any symptoms that might suggest COVID-19.

          For many experiments, participants might have to run an experiment that is based on code (i.e., the implementation of your restaurant software). It might not always be possible for them to run the code locally on their own computer remotely. If you prefer to not test participants in person due to Covid, keep the following alternatives in mind. Developing code that is easily deployed and shipped to others (e.g., in an .exe file) is too much work for this course and should not be your focus.

          You can think of these alternatives:
          • Can the code be used remotely?
          • Can you as a participant leader be a human proxy for the interaction with the code? For example, in a video chat (for example using Microsoft Teams) the participant can perhaps see the output from the software, they can verbally say what they would like to give as input, and the experiment leader types it on their behalf (Note: make sure to not bias the participant in their actions).
          • For some experimental set-ups, perhaps a "Turing test" like set-up is interesting, where participants see the input and output on a chat screen, but do not know whether this comes from a human or the software.
          • If your software does not work: can you perhaps use it in a so-called "Wizard of Oz set-up" where the human experimenter simulates the system’s response instead of having them interact with the software directly (perhaps do this via text chat, so the human user does not know that there is a "wizard" that fakes the system’s response)

        • Part 2c: Statistical Analysis


          In this week of the lab you should focus on data analysis and writing up, in particular the results and your general discussion.
          Based on your experimental design and with the data that you collected, you should now analyze your data and provide a statistical analysis. What you do will depend on the type of data you collected. In Appendix A some general resources are provided that can help you with conducting statistical tests. For example, the “stats flowchart” can be used to discover what test is appropriate for you, and the “statistics hell-p” page has detailed information on how specific tests work.

          Note that there are many ways to do statistical tests. In the examples below I use conventional (frequentist) hypothesis testing. Alternatives for such test, such as Bayesian analyses are also becoming increasingly popular a useful (for example, see JASP: https://jasp-stats.org/ ). These are not discussed here, but you are free to use those approaches if you are more familiar with those and if you can report on them appropriately.

          More often than not, you follow the following steps when conducting a statistical test:
          1. Forming a hypothesis: based on your previous work (design, step 2A) you should already have a hypothesis that you want to test with a statistical test.
            1. Remind yourself of what your hypothesis is and why that is your hypothesis.
            2. For example, a hypothesis might be: “Eating 1 bar of dark chocolate will increase happiness, compared to eating a bowl of yogurt.” Note how this hypothesis can be answered with “yes” or “no” and how there is an intervention (eating dark chocolate) that is compared to a control condition (eating yogurt) on a specific measure (score of happiness).
          2. Preprocessing of data: you preprocess your data such that you have some “clean” data on which you can conduct your statistics.
            1. For example, if you want to test how fast someone presses a button after a sound is played (i.e., their “reaction time”), you need to subtract the (standardized/absolute) time at which the button was pressed from the (standardized/absolute) time at which the sound was played to get the interval.
            2. You should ensure that after preprocessing, for each dependent variable (e.g., “reaction time”) you have 1 measurement per trial, per participant, and per condition (independent variable).
          3. Visual inspection of the data & assumption checking. Students (and researchers) often quickly move ahead and run a statistical test. It is often good to first look at your data to understand it, and to also check whether the assumptions that underlie your statistical test hold. If you made a mistake during data preprocessing (or data collection), your later statistical results are not meaningful.
            1. For example, is your data truly normally distributed (if you expect this), are the values that you measured all within an expected range and is there no missing data? For example, if you run a reaction time in which you expect reaction times between 0.5 and 3 seconds, it would be really strange if you find a negative value, or a very high value (say, 15 seconds). In this case, preprocessing might have gone wrong.
            2. What type of visualization is best for your experiment depends on your research question and your data. But you can for example consider a box-and-whisker plot (to understand the distribution of the data), or a barplot with error bars. Sometimes, merely plotting the raw data also helps.
          4. Visualizations for your report. Although the preceding focused on visualization to help testing assumptions, you often also want some visualization of your data in your paper for critical readers.
            1. For many experiments, researchers typically first calculate 1 average score value per participant per condition. That is, for each participant they calculate what the average performance was over all observations/trials, knowing that responses on individual trials might have been influenced by many aspects (e.g., compensating for noise in the measurement, see also section on design).
            2. So, imagine an experiment in which 40 participants each participated in two conditions (“difficult” and “easy”) and where we measured a reaction time on 30 trials per condition and per participant. You will then have 40 x 2 x 30 = 2400 datapoints. You first bring this down to the averages per condition per participant, so 40 x 2 = 80 data points.
            3. In your visualization you want to show what the mean is of the conditions (so, the mean for “difficult” and mean for “easy”) and also some measure of how the values are distributed across participants. For example, using an error bar that reflects a standard deviation, standardized error of the mean, or 95% confidence interval (each of these is calculated in different ways, and has different pro’s and cons for interpretation purposes; more information can be found in statistics resources, see Appendix B).
            4. For example, below is an example graph in which the mean and 95% Confidence Interval of participants’ reaction time are plotted in an “easy” and a “difficult” condition based on a fictitious experiment with 40 participants. Note that the means and the 95% CIs  are calculated using 40 datapoints (the averages of each participant per condition), not over the “raw” trial data.
            5. The nice thing about a graph like the one above (with means and 95% CIs) is that it can already provide you with an intuition about the outcome of your statistical test. In this case, the means are apart and the 95% CI intervals of the two conditions do not overlap, which suggests that the two underlying distributions are probably significantly different (if the “alpha” level is .05)  from each other and that the reaction time is probably significantly higher in the difficult condition compared to the easy condition. So, if your actual statistical test gives a very different outcome, you probably made some error and you should check your analysis code.
          5. Determining descriptive statistics. In your report you often also include some descriptive statistics about your experimental conditions, for example the means and standard deviations. These should again be consistent with what you found in your visual inspection.
          6. Conducting an inferential statistical test. The next step is then to use statistics to test whether there is truly a difference. As said before, within the context of this course a paired t-test is most likely appropriate.
          7. Reporting your result. You should now write up your result. Often this can be done very briefly. Note the use of italics and specific symbols below, which each have a meaning and reason (see also the APA publication manual if you are unfamiliar, see Appendix A). Note also that a unit is reported (seconds in s) for each measurement and that units are rounded to 2 decimals. For your data you should also think about  what an appropriate number of decimals is - don't simply “copy paste” of an outcome from your statistics program.
            1. This is an example of writing up the result associated with the graph you saw above and an example of a significant effect: “A paired t-test found a significant effect of difficulty on reaction time, t(39) = 4.032, p < .001. Reaction times were higher in the difficult condition (M = 6.43 s, SD = 1.76 s, 95% CI = [5.89, 6.98 s]) compared to the easy condition (M = 4.53 s, SD = 2.17 s, 95% CI = [3.86, 5.20 s]).
            2. This is an example of writing up a null result for a paired t-test with 20 participants: “A paired t-test found no significant effect of difficulty on motivation score, t(19) = 1.09, p = .29.” Make sure to not overinterpret a null-effect (for example first saying something is not significantly different statistically and then saying that the means differ. This is contradictory; hence means and standard deviations are not reported here).
            3. This is an example of writing up a “marginal effect” (an effect that just did not reach significance, conventionally when the p-value lies between .05 and .10): "A paired t-test found a marginal effect of difficulty on point score, t(19) = 1.99, p  = .056. The trend in the data was that point score was slightly higher in the easy condition (M = 8.0 points, SD = 1.5 points, 95% CI = [ 7.5, 8.6 points]) compared to the difficult condition (M = 7.3 points, SD = 1.3 points, 95% CI = [6.8, 7.8 points])." Side-note: do you see how the 95% CIs of the two conditions slightly overlap? That’s a sign that there might not be a statistically significant difference. Again, be cautious to not overinterpret a marginal effect.
          8. Interpreting your results and conducting additional analysis. If you have just 1 dependent variable and only a simple t-test, then a result is often easy to interpret. However, sometimes you have multiple dependent variables (for example, speed, accuracy, and subjective experience) and it is worthwhile to think about how these results “fit together”. For example, are faster participants less accurate? Such discussion of the general pattern is often done in an explicit section “discussion of results” of your report, or in the general discussion (see also how some example papers handle this, as referenced in Appendix C).

          Please know that you will not be graded on whether you find a significant result or not. However, we will assess how you report such a result and how you discuss it. For example, do you not misinterpret a null-result?

          Grading levels
          For each element you will be graded on the indicated grading criteria. Assessment is based on a 5-point scale, as follows:

          2. Barely sufficient
          3. Sufficient. We would expect the typical student to achieve this level.
          4. Above standard
          5. Outstanding. Meant for work that exceeds expectations by far. We expect to rarely award this.
          Your final score will be based on a weighted average of the scores across all criteria, and translated to a 10 point scale (not necessarily in a linear fashion).
          The lecturers will have a personal discretion to slightly round your grade up or down to better reflect the scores -- in particular for cases where there are scores on the extreme ends (outstanding or insufficient).

We do expect well-written documents of well-executed projects (within the constraints of the course). Make sure to be critical, take sufficient time for the writing, and proofread the work of your teammates.

          Title
          Give a title that is about the content (e.g., "A Computational Model of the Wason Selection Task"). The title should reflect the content/topic/research question of the report. Do not give a title that only reports what it is (i.e., do not use something like "Final Report of Methods in AI Research").

          Abstract (aim: 150 words)
          The abstract should be a summary of the entire project. It should not be just an introduction. The abstract should be around 150 to 200 words. It should address: what are you researching (research question), why are you doing this (theoretical/practical context, motivation, introduction), how are you doing this (method), what did you find (result), and what does this mean (discussion/implications).           Introduction and theoretical background (~1.5-2 pages)
          For the introduction, you should give an overview of: (1) what is the broader context or problem that you try to address, (2) what is a smaller, concrete problem or issue that you are addressing in your work, (3) why is this of theoretical or practical importance, and optional (4) a brief overview of how you are going to address the problem in the remainder of the paper. The introduction should form a coherent argument that leads to a clear, theoretically and practically supported motivation for the research approach (although some of the deeper theoretical motivation might follow in the theory section).
          After this general introduction, you should delve deeper into theory that is relevant, and do this in a structured manner. The theory section should place your project within the broader context of AI as discussed during the lectures and in the literature of the lectures (e.g., in the entry on AI in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy and Chapters 1, 2, and 16 from Brachman & Levesque).

          Comment on the relevance of your project for AI and address issues such as (A) its connection with other dialog systems, (B) its value for practical applications and/or (C) its merit for the understanding of human intelligence.

          Try to make a narrative around your project as part of the field of AI and don’t be afraid to express an opinion. For example, on the topic of human intelligence, and the question whether certain branches of AI research are relevant for modeling and understanding it, there is no universal agreement, and you are welcome to try formulate an interesting opinion on that matter. In the introduction the work should be embedded appropriately in a wider set of literature on relevant topics such as discussed in class (and potentially beyond that), for example the embedding of this research within the area of reasoning systems. Note however that not every area is equally relevant, i.e., not every topic that was covered in class should be covered in your report.

          General discussion (2 pages)
          The general discussion should address: (1) what was your research question and how did you address it, (2) if known, what are the implications of these findings for theory and/or practice (i.e. relate it back to the literature you reviewed in the introduction), (3) what are the limitations of your study and possibly how would you do this different if you had more time and/or resources (i.e., future work), and (4) what is your conclusion. The general discussion needs to be coherent with the introduction and results need to be related back to the literature in an appropriate and correct way. This discussion is the section where you can demonstrate that you have thought about the content of your project and results deeply. Try to make a narrative around your project as part of the field of AI and don’t be afraid to express an opinion (in fact: this is one way to demonstrate that you've thought deeply about the topic). The discussion should identify the limitations of the experimental set-up. Think about whether the identified limitations are indeed the most important.
          At the end of this section a concise conclusion should be presented that answers the research question. Note that in the context of this course it can also be the case that you can not answer the research question yet - for example due to null results or an inconsistent pattern of results - in which case your conclusion should reflect that there is no definitive answer. In that case you should clearly identify the following steps necessary to reach a more definitive conclusion.

          General writing tips
          All general guidelines for scientific reports apply. For example:
          • Always use your own words. Reports will be automatically checked for plagiarism. Plagiarism will be reported to the board of examiners and has serious consequences.
          • Make your message very explicit. Clear writing helps the reader understand your content better and faster.
          • Make use of a “writing plan”: Make a plan of what you want to write for each section before you start writing the actual sentences (e.g., which core arguments, what references/arguments/examples do you use?). The benefit of this approach is that your writing will contain more structure.
          • Start writing early on: writing a first sentence is often hard. So don't try to get every aspect of it perfect right away. Write what comes to mind. It is our experience that rewriting a text is a lot easier. So, make sure you start early, so you can get to the rewrite quickly.
          • Rewrite: academic papers always require some rewriting after the first draft. Leave sufficient time for rewriting of your text.
          • Make use of your team: If you get writer's block, pass a task on to a team member, or consult with them. Make sure everyone checks every section of the paper to get the message across clearly and to avoid unnecessary spelling or grammar mistakes.
          • Learn how to write by “meta-reading” other papers. See Appendix C.
